An Everclear exclusive
RYAN WALSH-MARTEL
STAFF WRITER

A couple Fridays ago Everclear came to Bowdoin to play the fall show in Morrell Gym. I had a chance to sit down with singer/songwriter and bandleader Art Alexakis before the show, for an Orient exclusive interview. The discussion ranged from the humorous to the serious, and Art showed himself to be an intelligent and deeply concerned man, but also someone who has not forgotten that he is a rock musician. He was an immediately likeable and unassuming guy; surprisingly soft spoken and delicate in his manner. Surprisingly short, he was dressed not like a 38 year old, but wearing baggy khakis a ratty sweater and Vans sneakers. He did not have any sort of "rock star" aura about him at all. He sat down on a couch in a room across the hall from Morrell Gym, put his feet up as we began our conversation.


Orient: What bands and albums do you listen to, who are your influences, and what bands do you draw your inspiration from?

Art Alexakis: Well, it depends on my moods, I have a lot of different tastes, I guess Beatles are pretty much a constant. Lately I've been listening to a lot of old country music, stuff I grew up with with my mom, and I listen to a lot of classic rock. I don't really listen to a lot of new music; it doesn't really strike me. I apologize if I seem really tired, I've been on a flight all night and it's not for any other reason, I just haven't had any sleep.

O: No problem. What is it about new music that doesn't hit you as hard as the stuff you listen to?

AA: I think everyone just sounds the same. It looks like a huge multi-million dollar game of follow the leader. You know. Everybody's trying to sound like everybody else or look like everybody else. It's very depressing, but I don't think it's gonna last, I think it's a phase, cause I think…like with the eighties with all the metal bands, and then something like Nirvana came along and opened the door for a lot of bands such as ourselves. People called it alternative rock, but I didn't think it was alternative. It was just rock and roll. And I think that's on a comeback and I'm looking forward to that.

O: I was just listening to your soundcheck and you really showed a diverse range of music. As Everclear has grown as a band, it what ways do you think your sound has changed?

AA: Well, you know, when Everclear started, I had just moved to Portland, and had been living in Portland for a little less than a year. I had a brand new baby and was living on welfare, I couldn't find a job in Portland, so I was very angry, and the music on our first album really reflects that. I think that now I'm still angry, but in different ways. In one way that's really different, and that's feeling more empowered because I've done something and achieved goals. That makes you feel a lot more empowered, setting goals and achieving them. But at the time, all the music that influences or are coming out in Everclear have always been there, they've always been things I've wanted to do, I just didn't feel confident enough in my capabilities and in our capabilities as a band. We feel stronger as a band and we feel like taking more of a leap, and getting in touch with things that we've always loved, such as R&B and pop, country, folk, and still Rock & Roll. My favorite bands like the Beatles, they never made records like they're making today that all sound the same, all the songs sound the same. It's like bands that say they are punk rock, the kids in the band weren't even born when punk rock came out. Had came and went, and now it's being brought back. To me punk rock never went away, because it's not about playing three chords real hard and fast, it's about your attitude you know? Fucking things up. Can I say that?

O: Yeah, I think so. It's a college paper.

AA: There you go.

O: I was reading an interview with you in Guitar World, and you were talking about studio work. You seem to be really into the recording aspect and getting everything just right. But also, elsewhere you were talking about how you love to play live shows. Which do you find more rewarding? Or is it different? Which do you enjoy more?

AA: Well, I'm in the studio right now producing another band, and I really like starting with an idea and watching it build and turn into a song, you know, I love that about recording. Live is a very, you know, more two or three-dimensional thing. When you make a recording you can put stuff in there and get more and more out of it every time you listen to it. Live it's just this one immediate thing, and you're in this zone playing, and that's very exhilarating and intoxicating. I don't do drugs or drink, I haven't in many years, but it's similar that buzz…when you're connected. When you're a fan of someone and you see it live it's just like [makes amazed facial expression]. I still feel like that, I went and saw Aerosmith on this tour they're doing, and met the guys backstage which was so cool, and watching them play songs, they played some new songs that I'm not too familiar with, but then they played stuff from the first four or five albums that I grew up with. They were like my banner. Them and Rush and Zeppelin and Sabbath, but Aerosmith and Rush were like my favorite bands, and Cheap Trick when they came out too. It's just exciting. Cheap Trick, same example, I got to play; we played with Rick Neilsen and Robin Zander. They got up and did Surrender with us at this movie premiere and it was just..we played hard and fast. Robin Zander was like, 'that is the hardest and the loudest and the fastest that song has ever been played. That's how it should have been recorded.' So, it was pretty cool.

O: You've spent a lot of time working with social issues, and you address them both in your songs and other actions, such as going to Capital Hill, and being involved in ACES, a child support enforcement program. While all of that is very admirable and is doing a lot of good, do you ever feel like, for example with Pearl Jam and Ticketmaster. You can't really write an article about them without mentioning Ticketmaster. Do you feel that way about your social involvement?

AA: Well, you know, I've got my own opinions on that, and I'm not one to ever bad talk other bands or criticize anyone. I think they did it to break a monopoly that wasn't going to be broken, and the people that suffered in the end, I mean the band suffered financially, but the people that really suffered were all their fans who didn't get to see them for all those years. They would only do sporadic shows without Ticketmaster. And I was just like cut the crap guys, you're a rock band. Get out and play your music and work a deal with Ticketmaster that they give so much money to charity or something, I mean Ticketmaster would have done that to avoid that thing, cause they weren't sure they were going to win that whole thing, which they did. Sometimes you got to lose a battle and win the war, but I see where you're going with your question. I don't think I've let anything I believe in or the band believes in to get in the way of the music, and hopefully it never will. I've never heard anyone come up to me and say 'oh you're that band that did that thing on Capital Hill.' It hasn't gotten to that yet, and there are still things I want to do for that organization and for other organizations. But it's about when you're doing well and you have extra: When you have plenty you should spread it around. Ultimately what comes first is my family and secondly my work and when those things are in tune then I feel happy as a person, and I believe that everyone's happiness is pretty much…when you're honestly happy, not selfishly happy. It's not about material wealth or the chicks or drugs or anything like that, but when you feel focused as a person you're gonna fit into society better. The happier that we can all make each other and still pull together and work together as a team, like this country has a lot in the last two weeks. [referring to the events of Sept 11.] I've been very amazed. But now I'm starting to hear that [Muslim people] are getting beat up in small towns, and that's just missing the whole point. It's like putting the Japanese in camps during World War II. These people are here because they want to be Americans, you know, Arab-Americans, German-Americans, and that's what makes our country awesome.

O: You perfectly anticipated my next question. What do you think about the political atmosphere in America today?

AA: Well, to be perfectly frank with you, it makes me a little nervous. I mean, when this thing happened; make no illusions, our whole world has changed. Economists are now saying 'yes we are in a recession' whereas two weeks ago they weren't saying that. I was looking at the economic indicators and I was looking at the stock market and saying this is nuts.. I am just amazed that this attack didn't drive us even farther into what could possibly be a depression. I mean, if our dollar gets devalued, people don't want to buy our dollar like they did two weeks ago. It's not strong anymore, it's medium to weak and when it gets weak, then our country goes into a depression. And that's when the rest of the world goes into a depression. The American dollar is the closest thing we have to a standard currency throughout the world. It's stronger than the pound, the yen, whatever. The weird thing about economics is that it's not just based on pragmatic stuff, it's based on what people think about the future, and speculation.

But getting, this is a roundabout answer for your question, getting back to the whole political thing, I'm all for supporting the President, although I don't fully believe he was rightfully elected the President of these country, but he is the President good or bad and I support him. But there's weird things going on with Executive Orders. There are challenges to the first and fourth amendments that we need to really be cognizant of and not get blinded by the love of our wonderful country and of our flag, but still be able to say hey, yeah let's punish the people that did wrong, let's do what we need to do, I'm behind you there, but don't take away my rights, even for a day. That's what I'm scared of. And I mean, well, I'm liberal to a sense but I'm not a card-carrying member of the ACLU. I do believe there's times when our civil liberties need to be suspended for the public good, that's fine, for a short amount of time. But sweeping executive orders that change lives are unacceptable. Do you know what I mean?

So it makes me a little frightened and it makes me wonder what's gonna happen. Wartime changes culture. It changes movies it changes music. A lot of the bands out there are what I call cookie monster bands. These loud new metal bands that are angry, but they aren't really angry about anything specifically, they're just pissed off. And I understand kids identifying with that because when you're a teenager you're just pissed off. Even kids from really good families with great parents are going 'god no one understand me.' Yeah, cause you're a fucking kid. Don't take yourself so seriously. Go have a good time. I just wonder if these bands are going to kind of get swept away because I think now people are going to want a connection. It could be like after World War II, when everyone was all ra ra ra, which I think this administration wants. Or it might go like Vietnam when we had protest songs, and the pop music was very sugary to counterbalance that, or it might be something of its own making. It's all exciting, but with that excitement there's a little bit of danger, so I'm just kind of waiting to see what will happen. I'm almost forty and I've seen a lot of shit in my life, and I think we're in a time where; we might be in this war for; how old are you?

O: 20
AA: You're draft age.

O: No problem.

CM: one hour

AA Really? they told me 5:30.

AA: Ok, I'm with you fellars. [Art looks back at me and starts talking] These college shows are always funny because it's like you're playing a sock hop in gym. You're always in a gymnasium, with just college kids. It's kind of a weird attitude but when the lights go down it's cool.

O: Bo-din
