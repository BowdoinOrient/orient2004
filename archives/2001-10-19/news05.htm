COURTESY OF SECURITY

After last year's fatal fire at Seton Hall University, in which 3 students were killed, the Bowdoin Community asked, "Could that have happened here?" The fact is, as last Friday's fire showed, it could happen anywhere.

According to the National Fire Prevention Association, approximately
1,400 structure fires occurred in school residence halls, fraternities, and sororities. The most common causes of fires in these residences are incendiary or suspicious causes. The second leading cause is cooking and the third is smoking.

With recent on-campus events, this is a good time to ask ourselves, "What is Bowdoin College doing to help protect life and property and preventing fires?"
Bowdoin's fire protection equipment is inspected regularly and the following items can be found in College residences:

Smoke detectors are located in all rooms, hallways, and lounges of College residences. If you smoke anywhere in the building, chances are the smoke detector will activate the fire alarm because they are very sensitive. If you hit one by accident while playing football in the hallway, it will activate the fire alarm as well.

Fire dlarms are located in each hallway of all College residences.
Try to be aware of where these pull stations are, because you never know when you might need them.

Fire extinguishers are located on all floors of dormitories and are encased in glass containers that should be broken only in case of an emergency. In apartment complexes, fire extinguishers are located in individual apartments. Again, it is necessary to be aware of the location of this equipment.

A few years ago, a fire at Brunswick Apartments destroyed an entire apartment. A lit candle sitting in a window tipped over and ignited the couch.
The student, who was home at the time, initially went to tell the proctor. She ran past the fire extinguisher in her apartment, past a fire pull station in the hallway, past the 'blue light' emergency phone on the quad, and to her proctor's apartment. Passing all of the fire prevention items that were in place wasted several minutes.

The best time to think of fire prevention is now, before it actually happens.
Sprinklers are located in most residence halls. Properly installed and maintained sprinklers prevent deaths outside the areas of origin in all but a few unusual situations. Property damage is 36% lower in living areas where sprinklers are present.

What can you do? When cooking, stick around. Don't leave food unattended. If something in the microwave catches on fire, keep the door closed and unplug it. Do not try to remove burning containers from a microwave.

Do not smoke in residence halls. (prohibited in student handbook)

Do not burn candles in the residence halls. (prohibited in student handbook)

What if you are caught in a fire? If you have to escape through smoke, crawl on the floor to your exit. The air is cleaner there. Test doorknobs and spaces around the door with the back of your hand for heat. The back of your hand is much more sensitive than the front. Use the stairs, never the elevator. If you are trapped, call the fire department and tell them where you are. Seal your door with wet rags and if possible signal your presence from a window.

Your building has an evacuation plan; learn it and participate in all drills. The residence halls also have a 'meeting point' outside of the building. If you don't know where this is, ask your proctor. Most importantly, if you hear an alarm, leave immediately. Don't wait to see if it is a false alarm. With today's improved technology we have very few 'false alarms'.

Each semester, Security conducts fire drills. We don't do this to irritate or disturb students. We do it because we know how important it is to know what to do in case of a real fire. And we do not want to see any loss of life from a fire at Bowdoin College.
